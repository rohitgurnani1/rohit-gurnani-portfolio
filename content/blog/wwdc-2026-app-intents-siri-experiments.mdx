WWDC 2026 didn't just update iOS and macOS — it reframed how Apple expects apps to talk to the system. I've been spending evenings with the new [App Intents documentation](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/appintents), the WWDC26 session videos, and sample projects — and the direction is clear: **Siri is becoming a context-aware action layer across apps**, not a voice shortcut menu.

If you build on Apple platforms (or you're curious where AI-native mobile is heading), this cycle is worth paying attention to.

## The shift: from SiriKit extensions to structured app actions

For years, Siri integration often meant a separate SiriKit extension, domain-specific intents, and a lot of ceremony for relatively narrow use cases. Apple's [App Intents framework](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/appintents) flips the model: you describe **what your app can do** and **what data it manages** in Swift, and the compiler generates metadata the system can discover at build time.

From Apple's docs:

> *"With App Intents, you express your app's actions and data in a structured way that makes them discoverable by Apple Intelligence and provides deeper integration with system features people use frequently."*

That "structured way" matters. Siri, Spotlight, Shortcuts, widgets, the Action button, and Focus modes aren't separate integrations anymore — they're **surfaces on the same App Intents foundation**.

WWDC 2026 pushed this further. Sessions like [Build intelligent Siri experiences with App Schemas](https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2026/240/) and the [Make your app available to Siri code-along](https://developer.apple.com/videos/play/wwdc2026/344/) walk through a new mental model: your app exposes **entities** (the things it knows about) and **intents** (the actions it can perform), and Siri routes natural language to those capabilities.

If you're still on SiriKit-only integrations, Apple's developer forums are blunt: Siri AI remains backward compatible with many SiriKit intents, but **you miss the new features** unless you adopt App Intent Schemas. The best path is dual support during transition — keep SiriKit for older devices, add App Intents for the new experience.

## App Schemas: teaching Siri your app's language

The most interesting WWDC 2026 addition, in my view, is **App Schemas** — predefined domains (calendar, messages, photos, and more) that map your app's functionality to actions Siri already understands.

Instead of training custom phrases from scratch, you conform to schema snippets. Apple's calendar code-along demo shows the pattern: scaffold an intent with the `@AppIntent` macro, adopt the schema parameters, implement `perform()`, and Siri suddenly knows how to **create events**, **open entities**, and **reason about your content** in domain-native language.

That's a big deal for third-party developers. You're not bolting voice onto your app — you're **registering your app in a shared action vocabulary** the system can compose across apps.

## Context is the product now

The old Siri experience often felt like: ask a question → get a card → done.

The 2026 documentation points somewhere richer:

- **On-screen awareness** — modifiers like `.appEntityIdentifier` and `.userActivity` help Siri resolve "this" and "that" against what's visible
- **Semantic indexing** — entities conforming to `IndexedEntity` show up in Spotlight with meaning, not just keywords
- **Cross-app content transfer** — exporting entities via `Transferable` and `IntentValueRepresentation` lets Siri hand off context between apps ("call this contact," "add this to my calendar") without every app reimplementing glue code

Apple describes this as content transfer: your app doesn't need to know what happens next — it needs to **describe its content accurately**. Siri and Apple Intelligence handle orchestration.

That's the evolution I find most compelling. We're moving from **app silos with voice triggers** to **a system that understands entities and routes actions across them**. Third-party apps stop being islands and start being nodes in a personal workflow graph.

## One definition, many surfaces

What kept clicking as I read the docs: App Intents isn't "the Siri API." It's the **integration layer for Apple Intelligence across the OS**.

The same intent might be invoked by:

- Siri in a multi-turn conversation
- Spotlight search
- A Shortcuts automation
- A widget or Live Activity
- The Action button on iPhone
- A Focus mode change

You implement once. The system exposes everywhere.

For indie developers and small teams, that's leverage. You're not maintaining six integrations — you're modeling your app's core verbs and nouns honestly, then letting the OS distribute them.

## What I tried in practice

I'm not shipping an App Store hit from these experiments yet — this is exploratory work between grad school and AI security projects. But the workflow I followed matches Apple's recommended path:

1. Read [Getting started with App Intents](https://developer.apple.com/documentation/appintents/getting-started-with-the-app-intents-framework)
2. Watch the WWDC26 **App Schemas** session for the conceptual map
3. Follow the **code-along** to wire entities, indexing, and a create-action intent
4. Skim the new **AppIntentsTesting** docs — validation without brittle UI automation is overdue

The documentation quality feels noticeably better this year. Less archaeology through stale forum threads, more end-to-end paths from "here's the concept" to "here's the Swift."

## Where I think this goes

Short term, the winners will be apps that expose **high-value, schema-aligned actions** early — the ones Siri can actually invoke when users ask naturally. Apple has reportedly lined up launch partners across ride-sharing, messaging, and media; the long tail of third-party apps will determine whether the new Siri feels magical or hollow.

Medium term, I expect **context stitching** to matter more than raw model size. The assistant that knows what's on your screen, what your calendar holds, and what your task app tracks — then acts across all three — wins on utility, not hype.

Long term, this looks like the mobile equivalent of what MCP is doing for agents on the desktop: **structured capabilities + shared context + policy boundaries**. I'm building MCPGuard for the agent tool-call layer; Apple's betting App Intents becomes the same layer for the personal OS.

Different stacks. Same architectural instinct.

## Honest caveats

A few realities kept me grounded while reading the docs:

- **Adoption is a chicken-and-egg problem.** Siri only feels smart if enough apps expose intents; developers only invest if users engage. WWDC 2026 is Apple's push to break that loop.
- **Migration takes real work.** Moving from SiriKit to App Intents isn't a weekend rename — entity modeling, schema conformance, and testing add up.
- **Regional rollout varies.** Developer APIs and consumer-facing Siri AI features won't land uniformly everywhere on day one — check Apple's platform availability notes for your target markets.

None of that diminishes the direction. It just means early movers who understand the docs will have a head start.
