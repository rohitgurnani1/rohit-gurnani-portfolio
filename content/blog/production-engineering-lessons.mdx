Before graduate school, I spent four years at Nordstrom shipping software used by millions of customers. It was the kind of environment that teaches you quickly: performance regressions become incidents, small UI bugs become brand moments, and "we'll fix it later" rarely stays later.

## Details are the product

At retail scale, polish isn't decorative — it's trust. A cart that hesitates, a search result that flickers, a checkout step that feels ambiguous: each one erodes confidence.

That mindset now shows up in everything I build, including AI tools. Users don't care that your RAG pipeline is clever if the interface feels sluggish or unclear.

## Reliability is a design constraint

One of the most valuable habits I picked up was treating reliability as a creative constraint rather than a backend concern. When you know a feature must survive peak traffic, you design simpler flows and clearer failure states up front.

That translates directly to AI products:

- Stream responses when possible
- Preserve partial results on failure
- Make uncertainty visible instead of hallucinating confidence

## Mentorship compounds

Moving from Engineer I to Engineer II meant less typing and more thinking — reviewing designs, unblocking teammates, and helping juniors avoid the mistakes I'd already made.

I still think the best teams optimize for **clarity over cleverness**. The code you can explain in one sentence usually survives longer than the code that impresses in a PR comment.

## Carrying it forward

Grad school opened a new axis — models, research papers, experiments — but the Nordstrom years grounded me in what actually matters when software leaves your laptop.

Build things that are fast, honest, and maintainable. Everything else is decoration.
