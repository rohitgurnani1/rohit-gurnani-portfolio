People ask why I left a stable engineering career for grad school. The short answer: I wanted to go deeper on the systems I was already building toward.

Retail engineering taught me how to ship. AI graduate study is teaching me **why things work** — and where they break.

## The gap I kept noticing

At Nordstrom, we used machine learning in recommendation, search, and operational tooling. But the distance between "model in a notebook" and "model in production" was always larger than the roadmap suggested.

I kept running into the same questions:

- How should we evaluate quality beyond offline metrics?
- Where do guardrails belong in the architecture?
- How do we keep latency acceptable when models get smarter and heavier?

Grad school gave me space to pursue those questions properly.

## What I'm focused on now

My MS in Artificial Intelligence at Texas A&M sits at the intersection of:

- Practical NLP and retrieval systems
- Product-minded engineering
- Tools that help developers move faster without cutting corners

That shows up in projects like MCPGuard and PromptGuard — both focused on making AI agent behavior safer without sacrificing developer velocity.

## Indie development still matters

I don't see school and indie building as separate tracks. Classes sharpen the fundamentals; side projects test whether ideas survive contact with real users.

Side projects like MCPGuard and PromptGuard, for example, started as ways to explore LLM security problems I kept running into in coursework and personal builds.

## Looking ahead

The next chapter isn't about choosing between research and product. It's about combining them — building AI systems that are rigorous underneath and effortless to use on top.

If that resonates with the kind of work you're doing, say hello.
